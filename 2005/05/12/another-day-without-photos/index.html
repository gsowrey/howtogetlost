Final Thoughts about Kazan

Filed under: Russia,Train Stuff,Uncategorized — Amy @ 2:11 am

Oh Kazan, how did I love thee? Let me count the ways…

Kazan was the most perfect day-long break from our crazy itinerary. We had bright blue skies, an atmospheric alley cafe in a hidden courtyard, glittering mosque domes, and an overall fabulous day.

Anyone who does the trans-sib or trans-mongolian should not miss Kazan! We couldn’t even get into the Kremlin because it’s under construction, but even without being able to see the buildings up close from inside the walls, it was still a fabulous day.

Just before getting on the train, we stopped by the “central market” about 200m south of the station. Our intention was to pick up some snacks for the train, and dinner, but in addition to a bag of peanuts, some candy, and bottled water, I ended up with 1kg of 2ply wool. Geoff hopefully got some good shots of the transaction. I was trying to ask if they had any really skinny wool yarn, but they thought I wanted 2ply instead of 3ply (which IS skinnier!), and anyhow, you’ll never guess how much the whole pack cost me…

100rubles.

About $4.00CAD or $3.00US.

Wow.

Wow. Wow. Wow.

So, next time Geoff is going to haul out the video camera and get the thing on tape too. Let’s just say there was a LOT of giggling from both sides!

The only problem with buying yarn in copious amounts is that one has to schlep it all the way across siberia, or pay loads of money to ship it home. I think I’m ok with shlepping. At least for now.

So, with that excitement behind us, we boarded the 378 train to Ekaterinburg – known as Sverdlovsk on the schedules – it’s Soviet name. This time, we weren’t instantly as lucky with great travelling companions. We had two guys – neither of whom knew each other or spoke english. Neither smiled at all until late the next day. The elder of the two purchased a sweet cake at one of the longer stops and offered to share. And then he smiled and everything was a lot more comfortable.

I am still not used to sleeping fully dressed on the train, especially when it’s so hot like it’s been. The one trick that really works for me is to take 2 Tylenol PM before tryin to go to sleep. It knocks me right out and I get a great night’s sleep.

Tonight’s train should be a bit more comfortable – it’s the number 56, and typically anything under 100 is named and considered the “flagship” of that particular route.

And if not, even last night was perfectly fine and much better than flying!

So, Ekaterinburg.

Our stay last night was the Academy of Geology Hotel. I predict Geoff will have a knack for describing the feats we went through to find the place, even after we found the building and courtyard it was in! The room was great, with a small fridge, shower, toilet, etc. And it was dead quiet. I think we were the only guests. Must still be the offseason, because we couldn’t find fault, except for the lack of hot water at night. (I did brave a cold shower last night though. It was worth it. I think.)

After freshening up, we went to a LP recommended restaurant – Trali-Bali. (It had the distinct advantage of bein 2 blocks from our hotel!). This is probably the first time we really couldn’t get along without the phrase book, as the menu was entirely in Russian. For some reason, everywhere else has either had photos (like at the Blini stands), or been translated. I’m proud to say that we managed to order 2 plates of grilled pork and 2 plates of blini with smoked salmon, my new favorite! All the food was incredible, and cheap! I think we paid around $15 each including some beverages.

Today we slept in and headed out at noon for the train station to leave our bags in the left luggage department and find an internet cafe. No luck with the cafe, we are back at the central post office, like in Kazan, with no way to download photos. It’s now 2pm, which is 2am at home. Sandra is online and we’ve been trying to get into some chat program for the last hour, it seems. So frustrating to be this close and not be able to communicate!

I’ll finish this entry with one last thought that’s been on my mind for the past few days. The further east we go, the friendlier we are findin the people. My theory is that some of the encounters we’ve had in the smaller places were probably equally “exotic” for the people we’ve met locally, since so many fewer Westerners actually come through this way. We’ve been lucky when we’ve needed help finding the hotel, etc, to run into people who do speak some English. However, I really wish I had made time for that Russian languae class. Being able to speak more than just the basics for thank you, please, hello, and goodbye, would be nice.

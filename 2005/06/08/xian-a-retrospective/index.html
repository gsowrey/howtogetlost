Xian – a Retrospective

Filed under: China,Uncategorized — Amy @ 10:03 am

Now firmly ensconced in Hong Kong, it seems odd, irreverant even, to be writing about Xian, an ancient capital city of mainland China.

But here I go.

And also, I have to add, I think that Geoff has only beaten me on the content front because he has posted many more photos than I have. Sometimes, this has been a connectivity issue. And sometimes, I’ve just been lazy or constrained by my 8MB of SDRAM. So I’ve committed this to be the entry with the most photos… I mean… ahem… why does everything have to be a competition?

So, where did I leave off?

Oh yes. It was raining in Xian. Raining a lot. My nice waterproof North Face jacket worked well, except for the parts that weren’t covered, like my legs and feet. Geoff had an umbrella from the hotel so he was in much the same position I was. Sopping wet from the knees down.

But eventually, the rain stopped and we were left with a lovely night for a walk around Xian’s historic (and mostly complete) city walls.

Our hotel was located 500m or so from the walls on the South side, so we walked down until we had crossed just under a gate, and then walked West until we found the massive South Gate.

Good lighting, eh? One thing I noticed almost immediately is that Xian is not nearly as accustomed to foreign visitors as Beijing and Shanghai, and even Tianjin. We got many many more stares and interested looks than ever before, especially when we pulled out our cameras and tripods for this shot.

The next morning, we headed out on our one and only group bus tour of this trip. The “Eastern” tour run by CITS goes to the famed Terra-cotta Warriors, just outside of Xian, but also stops at a few other key tourist sites. It’s a bit more pricey that doing it by public transport, but we were able to see way more than we could have covered in a single day on our own.

One of my major apprehensions is that we wouldn’t have enough time at the key sites on our own. Not so! The schedule felt very comfortable – a great mix between guided information and wandering time.

Our first stop was the “Big Wild Goose Pagoda”, 5km south of the city walls in Xian. This Buddhist temple complex was named after a Buddhist story that tells of starving monks who prayed and prayed for meat. One day, a big wild goose fell from the sky and died at their feet. They considered this a sign from the gods, a test, and from that day forward, became vegetarians.

The Pagoda itself has 7 stories. We climbed them all. I was told it was 264 stairs. It felt like 2,640,000. But who’s counting?

Surrounding the Pagoda are several smaller temple buildings and nice gardens, such as the ones shown here.

As usual, I took way more photos with my film camera. But I think you get the idea.

Next up was the Terra-cotta Factory on the outskirts of Xian. Our guide explained that we stop at the factory to see how the Terra-cotta warriors were originally made. I think it was a thinly veiled “shopping opportunity”, but I’m not really complaining.

They had several rows of life-size warriors. And, like the real thing that we were about to see, these warriors were also all unique.

Just ouside the door was a stack of old and unused molds. They looked pretty interesting.

We also got a quick lesson on how the warriors were made. These shelves have partially completed small souvenir warriors. I may have bought one or two. Maybe.

Admittedly, the next stop at the former Imperial Hot Springs wasn’t high on my list, but it was pretty, and it was a short stop. The spa looked really nice, but there wasn’t time for pampering.

And finally, the piece de resistance, the Terra-cotta Warriors.

The museum grounds are fairly vast. Three main pits of warriors have been opened to the public. Additionally, they have a building for a 360 “Circle-Vision” movie and small souvenir stands (of course). There’s also a museum-like building for housing additional rotating exhibits.

Going into the Pit 1 – the largest pit – it was easy to be overwhelmed by the number of visitors!

I eventually pushed and elbowed my way to the edge of the bar to take a few photos.

Really, these photos can’t possibly convey how breath-taking this sight was, so many rows of these life-size statues, set up in formation, ready for their General’s command.

It was easy to see why this is one of the Wonders of the world.

I don’t have a good photo of it, but the other two pits were left in mostly un-excavated form. (Probably because they are still working on them!) After seeing Pit 1, which has been reconstructed from the remains they found, it was pretty fascinating to see the smaller works-in-progress.

After another hour in the bus, we headed back to the hotel and then out for dinner – lunch had been provided as part of the tour but was utterly forgettable.

Instead of walking left out of the hotel as we had done the night before, we turned right and then right again down a streeet we had passed on the bus that morning.

We found our way into a two-story restaurant that was packed with locals – always a good sign. It turns out this was a “hot pot” restaurant. Originally a Mongolian dish prepared by nomadic soldiers in their metal helmets over a fire, the “hot pot” has definitely been adopted and modified by the Chinese.

We chose from a menu that was provided to us – in English – in a Microsoft Excel worksheet on someone’s laptop. I kid you not. It only had a few options, so we chose the “Aquatic Hotpot”, which I hoped meant a seafood stock, beef, shrimp, potato and radish.

The stock portion of the hot pot is brought to the table in a metal vat, which is placed on the gas burner at the table’s centre. The waiter turns on the gas and the stock boils in about 10 minutes.

Then, the waiter brings out the plates of food ordered. The beef was raw, the shrimp cooked, and the radish and potato partially cooked. It all goes into the pot. When it’s cooked, you pull it out, a piece at a time, and drop it in a cup of cooled broth and herbs until it’s cool enough to eat.

The end result is amazing. I don’t know why this hasn’t caught on in Canada! Or maybe it has, but I’ve never seen it.

And so ends our brief stay in Xian. I think it was a good choice to compliment the modernly-historic capital Beijing and the commercial and technological Shanghai. I do wish we had had more time to visit a smaller town in mainland China. At almost 7 million, I don’t think Xian qualifies.
